# How Arqma Wallet Compares

People usually arrive at Arqma Wallet already familiar with a different wallet, and the fastest way to understand what Arqma is (and isn't) is to compare it directly against what you already know. These comparisons are deliberately not sales pitches — Arqma isn't the right fit for every use case, and the honest answer says so.

## Arqma vs Monero (XMR)

The comparison people ask about most, because Arqma and Monero share direct lineage: Arqma is a 2018 community fork of the CryptoNote protocol family that Monero also belongs to. Both hide sender, receiver, and amount by default using the same core primitives — ring signatures, stealth addresses, RingCT.

Where they diverge: Monero has a dramatically larger network, market cap, and anonymity set, simply from being older and more widely traded — a bigger anonymity set means more real transactions to hide among. Arqma, in exchange, uses RandomARQ, a CPU-friendlier mining algorithm, and layers in a hybrid Proof-of-Work plus Proof-of-Stake system through Service Nodes that Monero doesn't have. The full breakdown is in [Arqma vs Monero (XMR) — what differs](https://arqmawallet.com/how-it-works/arqma-vs-monero).

## Arqma vs MetaMask

This comparison is less about which is "better" and more about recognizing they solve different problems. MetaMask is built for Ethereum and EVM-compatible chains — interacting with smart contracts, approving token spending, connecting to dApps. Arqma Wallet has none of that scope; it's a single-asset wallet for the ARQ chain with no smart-contract layer at all. If you need a wallet for DeFi or NFTs, that's a MetaMask job, not an Arqma one. [Arqma Wallet vs MetaMask — comparison](https://arqmawallet.com/how-it-works/arqma-vs-metamask) covers the scope difference in full.

## Arqma vs Trust Wallet

Trust Wallet is a multi-chain hot wallet operated by Binance, supporting a large number of assets under one app with a custodial-adjacent feel in places (Binance integration, in-app swaps). Arqma Wallet is the opposite on both axes: single-chain, fully non-custodial with no corporate operator, and intentionally narrower in scope. If breadth of supported assets is what you need, Trust Wallet covers more ground. If privacy-by-default on one specific chain, with no company involved anywhere in the stack, is the priority, that's the Arqma trade. See [Arqma Wallet vs Trust Wallet — comparison](https://arqmawallet.com/how-it-works/arqma-vs-trust-wallet).

## Arqma vs Proton Wallet

Proton Wallet approaches privacy from a different angle — a Bitcoin-focused wallet built by a privacy-first company (the makers of Proton Mail and Proton VPN) with an emphasis on ease of use and a polished, product-company experience. Arqma trades that company-backed polish for a community-run, protocol-level privacy model where the privacy is a property of every transaction on the chain itself, not an added layer. [Arqma Wallet vs Proton Wallet — comparison](https://arqmawallet.com/how-it-works/arqma-vs-proton-wallet) goes through both models side by side.

## Where Arqma fits among privacy wallets generally

Zooming out from one-to-one comparisons, [Best non-custodial privacy wallets in 2026](https://arqmawallet.com/how-it-works/best-privacy-wallets) places Arqma in the broader field of non-custodial privacy-focused wallets, with an honest look at where it's a strong fit and where a different tool — including some of the ones covered above — is the better choice for a given use case.

## The pattern across all of these

Every comparison above comes back to the same two axes: **scope** (single-chain privacy coin vs. multi-chain general-purpose wallet) and **custody model** (fully non-custodial, no company, vs. company-operated with varying degrees of custodial involvement). Once you know where you sit on those two axes, the right wallet — Arqma or otherwise — tends to be obvious.
